# Provenance Guard

An attribution-analysis backend for creative-sharing platforms. It classifies whether a
submitted piece of text was written by a human or generated by AI, returns an honest
confidence score, surfaces a plain-language transparency label, and lets creators appeal a
classification they believe is wrong.

**Guiding principle:** on a writing platform, wrongly labeling a human's work as
AI-generated is worse than missing AI. A false accusation damages a real creator; a missed
detection is a softer failure. That asymmetry shapes the thresholds, the label wording, and
the prominent appeal path throughout this system.

> Full design rationale — architecture narrative, diagram, and the five planning questions —
> lives in [`planning.md`](./planning.md).

## Detection signals — and why these two

The pipeline uses **two distinct signals**: one semantic, one structural. "Distinct" matters
— they fail on *different* inputs, which is what makes the pair more informative than either
alone. Two LLM prompts would both be semantic and would fail together.

**Signal 1 — LLM classification (Groq, `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`).** The text is sent to the
model, which returns a strict-JSON estimate of the probability it is AI-generated
(`{"ai_probability", "reasoning"}`). This captures *meaning* holistically — coherence,
register, and the even, hedged completeness typical of AI prose.
*Blind spot:* LLM-as-detector is unreliable and biased toward false positives on formal,
polished, or non-native-English human writing, and it is non-deterministic. This is the
biggest risk to a real creator and is why the whole system is tuned against false accusation.
(This blind spot showed up live in testing — see Known Limitations.)

**Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (pure Python).** Three structural metrics combined into
one AI-likeness score: sentence-length variance ("burstiness" — humans vary, AI is uniform),
type-token ratio (lexical diversity over a capped 200-word window), and punctuation variety.
*Blind spot:* structurally uniform human writing scores as AI — poetry with heavy repetition,
formal/academic prose, and very short texts (too few sentences to measure variance).

**Combination:** `confidence = 0.6 * llm_score + 0.4 * stylo_score`. The LLM is the stronger
holistic signal, so it carries more weight; stylometrics is an independent structural
cross-check that can pull an over-confident LLM back toward the middle.

## Confidence scoring & uncertainty

The score is `P(AI-generated)` on a single axis: `0` = confident human, `1` = confident AI,
**`0.5` = maximally uncertain** (the system telling the user it genuinely can't decide).
"Confidence" in the everyday sense is the *distance from 0.5*, not the raw number — which is
why `0.51` and `0.95` produce different labels (barely-off-the-fence vs. a strong AI signal).

Thresholds are **asymmetric on purpose**:

| Combined confidence | Attribution     |
|---------------------|-----------------|
| `< 0.40`            | `likely_human`  |
| `0.40 – 0.65`       | `uncertain`     |
| `> 0.65`            | `likely_ai`     |

The AI cutoff (0.65) sits 0.15 above the neutral midpoint; the human cutoff (0.40) only 0.10
below it. We require more evidence to accuse than to clear, so borderline cases default to the
less-harmful outcome.

### Calibration test — four deliberately chosen inputs (actual run)

Both individual signal scores and the combined score were logged for four inputs spanning the
range. These are the real results from a live run against Groq:

| Input | `llm_score` | `stylo_score` | `confidence` | Attribution | Reading |
|-------|-------------|---------------|--------------|-------------|---------|
| Clearly human (casual review) | 0.20 | 0.22 | **0.208** | `likely_human` | Correct — cleared easily. |
| Clearly AI (formal AI essay) | 0.80 | 0.357 | **0.6227** | `uncertain` | Signals disagreed; the system hedged rather than accuse. |
| Formal human (monetary policy) | 0.80 | 0.50 | **0.68** | `likely_ai` | **False positive** — the LLM over-flagged formal writing. |
| Lightly-edited AI (remote work) | 0.40 | 0.304 | **0.3614** | `likely_human` | **False negative** — light editing evaded detection. |

The scoring produces meaningful variation, not a constant, and all three labels are reachable.
Just as importantly, the two errors point in the directions the design predicts: the system
errs toward *clearing* (the lightly-edited AI was let through) rather than *accusing*, and the
one false positive is on exactly the kind of writing the appeal path exists to protect (below).

### Two example submissions

**High-confidence case — clearly human:**
```
input:  "ok so i finally tried that new ramen place downtown and honestly? underwhelming..."
llm_score: 0.20   stylo_score: 0.2199   confidence: 0.208   ->  likely_human
```

**Lower-confidence case — clearly-AI text that the signals disagreed on:**
```
input:  "Artificial intelligence represents a transformative paradigm shift in modern society..."
llm_score: 0.80   stylo_score: 0.3568   confidence: 0.6227  ->  uncertain
```

The first is far from the 0.5 midpoint (high certainty it is human); the second sits near it
(low certainty) because the LLM flagged it but stylometrics read it as structurally varied — so
the combined score landed in the uncertain band instead of flipping to an extreme.

## Transparency label — three variants (exact text)

**`likely_human` (confidence `< 0.40`)**
> ✓ Likely human-written. Our analysis found writing patterns consistent with human
> authorship, and no strong signals of AI generation. This is an automated estimate, not a
> guarantee.

**`uncertain` (confidence `0.40 – 0.65`)**
> ● Origin uncertain. Our analysis was inconclusive — this content shows a mix of signals,
> and we can't determine with confidence whether it was written by a person or generated by
> AI. Please treat this result as informational only.

**`likely_ai` (confidence `> 0.65`)**
> ⚠ Signals consistent with AI generation. Our analysis found patterns often associated with
> AI-generated text. This is not a definitive judgment, and automated detection can be wrong —
> especially for formal or non-native-English writing. If you wrote this yourself, you can
> appeal.

The AI variant never says "this was made by AI"; it says "signals consistent with," names its
own known weakness, and offers the appeal — the false-positive asymmetry expressed in the UX.

## Appeals workflow

A creator appeals with the `content_id` from their submission plus free-text
`creator_reasoning`. On receipt the system: (1) locates the original decision, (2) updates that
content's status **in place** to `under_review`, (3) **appends** a new audit-log event with the
reasoning and a reference to the original attribution, and (4) confirms receipt. The original
classification entry is never modified — the audit log is append-only, so the record shows both
the decision and the contest side by side. No automated re-classification occurs.

The logged appeal below is on the real false positive from testing: the formal-writing
paragraph flagged `likely_ai`, appealed by a creator identifying as a non-native English
speaker — the exact scenario the asymmetry and the appeal path are designed for.

## Rate limiting

`10 per minute; 100 per day`, keyed by client IP (`flask-limiter`, in-memory storage).

- *10/minute* accommodates a real writer publishing a batch (e.g. a poetry collection) in one
  sitting, while blocking rapid-fire flooding.
- *100/day* caps sustained scripted abuse — a bot probing the classifier would exceed this
  quickly.
- *IP-keying* is used because there is no auth in this prototype. **Limitation:** IP-keying is
  crude (shared IPs, VPNs, NAT); production would key by authenticated `creator_id`.

**429 evidence** (12 rapid requests against a 10/minute limit — ten `200`s, then `429`s):

## Audit log

Every decision and appeal is written to a structured SQLite audit trail. Two tables: `content`
(current state per submission; `status` updated in place on appeal) and `audit_log`
(append-only event history — never updated or deleted). `GET /log` returns recent events.

Each event records: `content_id`, `event_type` (`classified` / `appeal`), `timestamp`,
`attribution`, `confidence`, `llm_score`, `stylo_score`, `status`, and `appeal_reasoning`.

## Known limitations

These are real behaviors observed in the calibration run, each tied to a property of the signals
— not generic "needs more data" caveats.

- **False positives on formal / non-native-English writing.** The formal monetary-policy
  paragraph scored `likely_ai` (confidence 0.68): the LLM signal returned 0.80 on writing that
  is uniform, complex, and formal — its documented blind spot. This is the population a false
  accusation harms most, which is why detection is tuned conservatively, the AI label is hedged,
  and the appeal path is prominent. The single logged appeal is this exact case.
- **False negatives on lightly-edited AI.** The lightly-humanized AI paragraph scored
  `likely_human` (0.3614): once AI text is edited to add small irregularities, both signals
  soften and the system errs toward clearing. This is the *intended* direction of error on a
  writing platform (missing AI is softer than accusing a human), but it means the system is not
  a reliable gate against determined evasion.
- **Signals can disagree, and the system then hedges.** The clearly-AI essay scored `uncertain`
  (0.6227) because the LLM flagged it (0.80) but stylometrics read it as structurally varied
  (0.357). Strong AI text was not confidently caught — an honest consequence of combining a
  semantic and a structural signal that sometimes point in opposite directions.

## Spec reflection

- *Where the spec helped:* writing the three label variants verbatim in `planning.md` before
  any code made the label-generation function trivial — a direct lookup against text that
  already existed, with no last-minute wording decisions.
- *Where implementation diverged:* the plan anticipated possibly needing a separate calibration
  step to map raw signal outputs onto the confidence score. In practice both signals already emit
  `[0,1]` probabilities, so a weighted average was directly interpretable and no extra calibration
  layer was built — the divergence simplified the system without weakening it.

## AI usage

- **Confidence scoring — directed and overrode.** The AI's first-pass scoring averaged the two
  signals evenly (0.5 / 0.5) and centered the label thresholds symmetrically at 0.5. I overrode
  both: weighted the LLM 0.6 / stylometrics 0.4 (the LLM is the stronger signal), and set
  **asymmetric** thresholds (0.40 / 0.65) so the system requires more evidence to accuse a human
  than to clear one — reflecting the false-positive asymmetry the project centers on.
- **Graceful degradation — directed a change.** The first LLM-signal implementation let a Groq
  API error propagate and 500 the request. I directed the AI to return a neutral `0.5` fallback
  on any API/parse failure instead, so a transient error can't crash a submission or a demo.
